Why?

While taking long exposure photos of the stars, you encounter a problem where the stars appear to move across the sky. This is due to the rotation of the Earth. This project aims to solve this problem by using a camera with a wide angle lens and a motorized mount to track the stars.

What the Pros Use

Most deep sky photographers use a motorized mount to track the stars. These mounts are expensive, and I wanted to make my own. The most common type of mount is the equatorial mount such as the SkyTracker Pro. The other mount types are alt-azimuth and polar, but they are not as accurate as the equatorial mount.

The cheapest type of equatorial mount is a "barn-door" mount, such as the Nyx Tracker. Because of the simplicity of a "barn-door" style mount, this is the design I went with.

How it Works?

By using a hinge to join two pieces of wood, it is possible to create a straightforward single-arm barn door tracker. On the top board is a camera affixed, typically with a ball joint to allow the camera to be directed in any direction. A threaded rod or bolt is often turned to force the boards apart (or together) at a steady rate after the hinge is positioned in relation to a celestial pole.

An example of this is a tangent drive. When utilizing a 50 mm lens, this style of mount is effective for about 5 to 10 minutes before tracking issues are noticeable. The tangent mistake is the cause of this. When employing an isosceles mount, that time can be extended to roughly 20 minutes.

Results

With the tracker, I was able to get some pretty promising results. The first photo is with out the tracker and the second is with the tracker.

There is a clear reduction in the star trails, but the stars are still not as sharp as I would like. I think this is due to the fact that I don't have a reliable way of aiming the tracker. I am currently working on a way to do this.
